    Make a granny square

    In this tutorial, you’ll make a crochet granny square with a single colour or skein of yarn.

    A yellow crochet granny square with three rows, made using one continuous length of yarn.
    Source: Sarah Maker

    Before you start

    This tutorial assumes that you already know the following:

    • US crochet terminology (see Granny square glossary)
    • Chain stitch (ch)
    • Double crochet (dc)
    • Slip stitch (sl st)

    You need the following equipment:

    • A cotton or acrylic yarn of your choice (Tip: chunkier yarns are better for learning)
    • A crochet hook at the right size for your yarn (check the yarn label, or use a wraps-per-inch converter tool)
    • A set of stitch markers (you need at least five)

    Make a crochet granny square

    Because this is a beginner tutorial, I’ll provide plain English instructions alongside the standard crochet notation.

    Row 0 makes your starting circle.

    Row 0: ch 4, sl st into the first chain, put stitch marker in ch4 sp

    Plain English:

    1. Make a slip knot to attach the yarn to your crochet hook, and make 4 chains.
    2. Slip-stitch into the first chain you made. This should make a closed circle of 4 chains.
    3. Put a stitch marker in the circle, so that you can identify it later.

    Row 1: ch 3 (counts as first dc here and throughout), 2 dc in ch4 sp, [ch 2, 3 dc in ch4 sp] * 3, ch 2, ss to 3rd st in starting ch. (stitches: 12dc, 8ch)

    Plain English:

    1. Chain 3. This creates your first double crochet, and gets your working yarn to the right height to create your first row.
      (Note: Always place a stitch marker in the third chain you make, because you’ll need it later.)
    2. Make 2 double crochets into the circle you made in Row 0. This is different to regular crochet: you’re not crocheting into the stitch, but underneath it. You should now have 3 double crochets in total (known as a “granny cluster”), making the first side of your first square.
    3. Chain 2 (this makes a corner), then make another granny cluster into the circle from Row 0 (this makes another granny cluster).
    4. Repeat step 3 twice more. You should now have 3 corners, and 4 granny clusters.
    5. To make your final corner and close the row, chain 2 and then slip-stitch into the 3rd chain from your original starting stitch.
    6. Lay your piece out flat. You should have 4 granny clusters making up the sides of your square, and 4 chain-2 spaces that make the corners.
    7. Place a stitch marker in each corner, to help you identify them while you work the next row.

    Next, let’s make that square bigger. Row 2 works into the chain-2 spaces of Row 1. In each corner, we’ll add a granny cluster, a chain-2 space, and another granny cluster. Remember that you’re always working into the spaces between granny clusters, and not into the granny clusters themselves.

    Row 2: ch 3, sk 3dc, [(3dc, ch 2, 3dc) in ch2 sp] * 3, (3dc, ch2, 2dc) in ch2 sp, ss to 3rd st in starting ch3. (stitches: 24dc, 8ch)

    Plain English:

    1. Chain 3 and place a stitch marker in the third chain you make. On this row, this chain-3 will be the last double crochet in the final granny cluster you make (this will make more sense in step 5).
    2. In the next chain-2 space, make a granny cluster, then chain 2, then another granny cluster in the same space. This creates your first corner for this round.
    3. Repeat step 2 twice more. You should now have 6 granny clusters, and 3 corners.
    4. To make your final corner and close the row, make a granny cluster in the final chain-2 space, then chain 2, then make 2 more double crochets.
    5. Finally, slip-stitch into the 3rd chain from your original starting stitch. This should complete the final granny cluster.
    6. Lay your piece out flat. You should have 8 granny clusters making up the sides of your square, and 4 chain-2 spaces that make the corners.
    7. Move your stitch markers to the new corners.

    Finally, let’s add one more row. Here, you’ll be thinking about sides as well as corners.

    Row 3: ch 3, 2 dc in empty sp, (3dc, ch 2, 3dc) in next ch 2 sp, [3dc in empty sp, (3dc, ch 2, 3dc) in next ch 2 sp] * 3, ss to 3rd ch in starting ch3. (stitches: 36dc, 8ch)

    Plain English:

    1. Chain 3 and place a stitch marker in the third chain you make. On this row, this chain-3 is the first double crochet in the first granny cluster you make.
    2. In the empty space under your chain-3, make 2 more double crochets. This makes your first granny cluster for this row.
    3. In the next chain-2 space, make a granny cluster, then chain 2, then make another granny cluster. This creates your first corner for this round.
    4. In the next empty space, make a granny cluster.
    5. In the next chain-2 space, make a granny cluster, then chain 2, then make another granny cluster.
    6. Repeat steps 4 and 5 twice more. You should now have 12 granny clusters, and 4 corners.
    7. To close the row, slip-stitch into the 3rd chain from your original starting stitch.
    8. Lay your piece out flat. You should have 12 granny clusters making up the sides of your square, and 4 chain-2 spaces that make the corners.
    9. Move your stitch markers to the new corners.

    You’re done! You should have a completed, fully symmetrical granny square with three rows, creating a checkerboard effect.

    Making your granny square bigger

    You can make your granny square as large as you want, as long as you continue to follow the same pattern shown on Row 3, and follow the basic rules:

    • On the sides of your square, make a granny cluster in an empty space, and then skip over to the next empty space.
    • In the corners of your square, make a granny cluster into the chain-2 space, then make 2 chains, then another granny cluster into the same chain-2 space. Then go back to making sides until you reach the next corner.
